Related papers Working Paper on Fiction and International Relations Theory Paul Musgrave Abstract: Many researchers assert that popular culture warrants greater attention from international relations scholars. Yet work on IR and popular culture remains marginal. Although many reasons for this inattention are not justifiable, some are. In particular, scholars have not yet adduced a theoretical answer for why fictional sources might systematically or significantly influence actors’ real-world behavior. Drawing on work from other disciplines, including cognitive science and psychology, we propose a theory of how fictional narratives can directly influence real actors’ behavior. As people read, watch, or otherwise consume fictional narratives, they process those stories as if they were actually witnessing the phenomena those narratives describe, even if those events may be unlikely or impossible. These “synthetic experiences” can change beliefs, reinforce preexisting views, or even displace knowledge gained from other sources (including scholarship) for elites as well as mass audiences. Because ideas condition how agents act, we argue that international relations theorists should take seriously how popular culture propagates and shapes ideas about world politics. We demonstrate the plausibility of our theory by examining the reception and influence of the U.S. novelist Tom Clancy.
