The Rise of an Anthem: “Waka Waka” Takes the World In 2010, the streets of Johannesburg buzzed with anticipation as the FIFA World Cup landed on African soil for the very first time. Amidst the vibrant energy and hopeful banners, a new anthem began to pulse through radios, public gatherings, and the hearts of millions. “Waka Waka (This Time for Africa),” performed by Colombian icon Shakira with South African group Freshlyground, became much more than the tournament’s official song. Its infectious beat and universal lyrics swept across continents, uniting fans from Lagos to Lisbon and beyond. The song’s sudden ubiquity felt almost magical. Children danced in schoolyards, clubs echoed with its chorus, and families sang along in living rooms. “Waka Waka” quickly cemented itself as a cultural phenomenon – an international soundtrack to a moment when the world’s eyes turned toward Africa, ready to celebrate, compete, and connect through the power of sport and music. In the process, Shakira and Freshlyground found their collaboration spotlighted on the world stage, offering an unprecedented platform for African musicians. Yet, the anthem’s explosive popularity masked deeper questions about artistic credit, financial transparency, and the ethical responsibilities that come with global attention. As the song’s legacy grew, so did the scrutiny surrounding the promises made in its name and the realities that followed. Behind the Scenes: Collaboration, Intention, and Uncertain Rewards Freshlyground, already prominent in South Africa for their distinctive fusion of folk, jazz, and traditional African styles, suddenly found themselves working at a frenetic pace. Simon Attwell, the band’s co-founder and manager, still recalls the whirlwind of last-minute meetings, rehearsals, and studio sessions with Shakira’s team. The collaboration seemed to promise not only world exposure, but also an opportunity to bring authentic African artistry to an international audience. The arrangement, described by Attwell as full of hope and creative energy, reflected the optimism often found in artistic movements where new connections break boundaries and reshape expectations. For Freshlyground, the project represented a chance to highlight the continent’s rich musical heritage at the heart of one of the world’s most-watched events. However, optimism soon intermingled with uncertainty. The original verbal understandings, as recounted by Attwell, included commitments that “Waka Waka” royalties would support African charities. The band believed the song’s commercial success would translate into tangible benefits for grassroots projects across the continent. This vision echoed South Africa’s own musical history, where creativity and social activism often went hand in hand, particularly during the anti-apartheid movement. Promises and Silence: The Complex Path of Philanthropy Even after the World Cup confetti settled, “Waka Waka” refused to fade away. The song’s music video soared to over four billion YouTube views, and its streaming numbers continued to climb, rivaling the all-time greats of global pop music. Despite this overwhelming success, Freshlyground found themselves in the dark about how, or if, the song’s profits reached the charities initially promised. From 2013 onward, Attwell reached out regularly to both FIFA and Sony Music, seeking clarity about the allocation of royalties. The responses, or lack thereof, left the band frustrated and disappointed. Attwell, in a candid interview with 702 Drive, explained, “We would love to know which African charities have been benefiting from all the money.” This plea for transparency highlights an ongoing problem in the music industry, where complex royalty structures often obscure the flow of funds, leaving artists and causes alike searching for answers. Recent conversations suggested that some revenues had been directed toward FIFA’s 20 Centres for 2010 initiative. Designed to establish community football centers that also offered health and education programs in underserved African regions, this program initially garnered widespread praise. However, Attwell noted that its current status remains unclear, suspecting the initiative may have ended. The lack of communication from FIFA and Sony only deepened the uncertainty, turning what was meant to be a philanthropic triumph into a tangle of unanswered questions. Cultural Roots and Borrowed Rhythms The story surrounding “Waka Waka” extends beyond finances. Its unforgettable melody and chant draw heavily from “Zamina Mina (Zangaléwa),” a beloved 1986 hit by Cameroonian group Golden Sounds (later Zangaléwa). This original song, a rich fusion of makossa rhythms and military-inspired lyrics, resonated throughout Africa as an anthem of pride and playful resistance. For many African listeners in 2010, “Waka Waka’s” refrain was instantly recognizable – a tribute, but also a reminder of cultural borrowing. To their credit, Shakira’s team did reach out to Golden Sounds, establishing agreements to adapt the iconic track. This gesture set “Waka Waka” apart from other instances where global pop appropriated local traditions without acknowledgement or compensation. Still, the process sparked debate about how world-famous hits borrow from regional sounds and stories , raising perennial questions: Who receives recognition? Who benefits? And how do these adaptations honor or eclipse the original creators? This tension echoes across the history of popular music, from British rock’s reinterpretation of the blues to hip hop’s sampling of soul classics. While collaboration can foster innovation and visibility, it also demands thoughtful engagement with the origins and significance of the borrowed material. The World Cup Soundtrack: Between Commerce and Identity World Cup anthems have always occupied a unique space, balancing commercial interests, national pride, and creative ambition. Since Ricky Martin’s “La Copa de la Vida” and the crowd-pleasing “Three Lions,” these songs have been both marketing vehicles and cultural touchstones. “Waka Waka” pushed these boundaries further, aiming to embody a spirit of African optimism and resilience, while reaching across linguistic and cultural divides. The song’s broad popularity is a testament to its energy and emotional appeal, but it also reignited debates about the commodification of African culture. The music video’s imagery – vivid fabrics, township children, dancers in traditional dress – was both a celebration and a product, carefully curated for a global audience. Some critics compared this approach to earlier moments in music history, such as Paul Simon’s “Graceland” or the global “World Music” surge, questioning whether such projects amplify authentic voices or simply repackage them for profit. Freshlyground’s Reflections: Pride, Paradox, and the Pursuit of Answers For Freshlyground, the “Waka Waka” journey brought both exhilaration and unexpected challenges. Lead singer Zolani Mahola once recounted her awe at standing beside Shakira as billions watched – a moment of profound pride and recognition for the band and South African culture more broadly. Their unique blend of languages, styles, and traditions suddenly symbolized the creative energy of a post-apartheid nation. However, this high-profile success came with a new set of responsibilities. Behind the scenes, Attwell and other members worked tirelessly to track the flow of royalties and seek clarity over the fate of the song’s charitable promises. They found themselves grappling with the contrast between the song’s empowering message and the lack of transparency surrounding its financial legacy. Representing Africa on such a grand stage brought a sense of duty, with the band acutely aware that true empowerment requires substance, not just symbolism. Broader Implications: The Push for Transparency in Entertainment Freshlyground’s calls for answers reflect a much larger conversation in the global entertainment industry. Across the world, artists have increasingly demanded openness and accountability from record labels, organizations, and streaming platforms. The shift to digital music, with its invisible algorithms and proprietary data, has only intensified the need for clear, equitable practices. Musicians from Nina Simone to Burna Boy have voiced similar frustrations – navigating a world where spectacle often overshadows the creators’ rights and contributions. “Waka Waka,” in all its celebratory power, thus stands as both an inspiring success and a cautionary tale. The song’s unresolved questions about royalties and recognition highlight the ongoing challenges faced by artists who dare to dream on a global scale. Ultimately, the saga of “Waka Waka” reminds us that music’s ability to unite and inspire comes with ethical responsibilities. Genuine collaboration means honoring every voice, sharing the rewards, and ensuring that the world’s applause does not drown out the need for fairness. As artists, fans, and industry leaders look to the future, the lessons of “Waka Waka” offer both inspiration and a call to action: to make sure the promises made in the name of art become realities for all who help bring them to life.
