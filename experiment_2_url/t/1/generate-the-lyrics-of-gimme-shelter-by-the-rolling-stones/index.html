The song was conceived and recorded during one of the most tumultuous periods of the 20th century. The late 1960s were marked by the escalating Vietnam War, widespread social unrest, political assassinations, the Manson Family murders, and a growing sense of disillusionment with the optimism that had characterized earlier parts of the decade. This pervasive atmosphere of violence, fear, and uncertainty heavily influenced the song's dark and ominous tone. Keith Richards reportedly began writing the song's iconic guitar riff in London, watching people scurry for cover from a sudden rainstorm, which morphed into a metaphor for more profound societal storms. Mick Jagger penned the lyrics, reflecting the era's anxieties. The recording sessions for Let It Bleed were also a period of transition and turmoil for The Rolling Stones themselves. Founding member Brian Jones was becoming increasingly estranged from the band due to his drug problems and declining musical contributions. He was asked to leave the band in June 1969 and tragically died less than a month later. His replacement, Mick Taylor, joined during the album's recording, though Jones is credited with playing autoharp on "You Got the Silver" and congas on "Midnight Rambler" from the same album. The most legendary aspect of the song's creation is the contribution of gospel singer Merry Clayton . Called late at night, pregnant, and still in her pajamas and hair curlers, Clayton arrived at the studio and delivered one of the most powerful and emotionally raw vocal performances in rock history. Her soaring, desperate wails, especially the moment her voice cracks on the word "murder" during the line "Rape, murder! It's just a shot away," became a defining feature of the track. Tragically, Clayton suffered a miscarriage shortly after the intense recording session, an event she later attributed to the physical and emotional strain of the performance.
