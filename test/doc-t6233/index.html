The song is about being torn between two loves - the people in your life, and the thing that makes you most yourself. It's about knowing that you love doing something *so much* that you will keep leaving your family behind, even though you love them more than (almost) anything else. In the Ray Bradbury story, main character asks his father, the Rocket Man of the title, what it's like out in space. '“It’s the best thing in a lifetime of best things.” Then he caught himself. “Oh, it’s really nothing at all. Routine. You wouldn’t like it.”' Later, he tells his son, '“Don’t ever be a Rocket Man...I mean it,” he said. “Because when you’re out there you want to be here, and when you’re here you want to be out there. Don’t start that. Don’t let it get hold of you.”' The singer is spending all his enthusiasm "out there alone" - he adores space, but it uses him up, and leaves him with less of himself to give his family. The moment when he's most himself is something he CANNOT share with the people he loves, and it's tearing him in two. Could the song ALSO be about rock stardom? Easily. Could it be about being gay in a homophobic world? Certainly. Could it be about doing drugs? ...I find that one harder to imagine. The song has such a sense of wonder, and a soaring joy - I don't think the metaphor works as well if the thing pulling the singer away is something self-destructive, rather than something incredible and amazing, but exhausting and draining. To be a Rocket Man is to feel the loneliness of never being able to share your truest self with your loved ones, even though you desperately want to, and they truly (and equally desperately) want you to. It's tragic and achingly beautiful at the same time. John from Romeoville The lyrics were inspired by The Rocket Man by Ray Bradbury. The lyrics in the song, inspired by a short story of the same title written by Ray Bradbury, and written by John's longtime collaborator Bernie Taupin, describe a Mars-bound astronaut's mixed feelings at leaving his family in order to do his job. Musicmama from New York, Ny It's interesting that David Bowie's "Space Oddity," which was released three years before "Rocket Man," became popular in the US at the same time as "Rocket Man." In both songs, space travel is used as a metaphor. But, to me, the real subjects of both songs are displacement and alienation. You hear it in lines like "It's just my job five days a week" in "RM" and "Planet Earth is blue and there's nothing I can do" in "SO". I could cite other examples, but you get the idea: People are just going through the motions; the enthusiasm and giddiness of youth have turned into the hangover of being a middle-aged functionary. These themes of displacement and alienation are also found in another song that became wildly popular around that time: Don McLean's "American Pie." That song's narrator is just as out of place and feels just as isolated in the post-Elvis world as David Bowie's and Elton John's personae feel in outer space. They're all great songs. RM has the prettiest melody. AP has the greatest emotional depth. Musically, I think SO is the most complex and interesting. I love them all.
