Instead, it delves into darker themes of obsession, surveillance, possessiveness, and control . Sting himself has described the song as "very, very sinister and ugly," reflecting feelings of jealousy and ownership after a painful breakup. "Every breath you take Every move you make Every bond you break, every step you take I'll be watching you." (The song's iconic, yet unsettling, refrain) The lyrics meticulously list actions of the subject ( "every move you make," "every vow you break," "every smile you fake" ) and pair each with the narrator's unwavering, almost menacing, promise: "I'll be watching you." This constant observation creates an atmosphere of suffocation and lack of privacy, more akin to stalking than romantic devotion. The line "Oh, can't you see, you belong to me?" explicitly reveals the possessive nature of the narrator's feelings. The song's widespread misinterpretation as a tender love ballad, often played at weddings, is a testament to its smooth musical arrangement and the ambiguity that can arise when dark lyrics are set to a pleasing melody. The song was written by Sting during a period of intense personal turmoil, specifically the collapse of his marriage to actress Frances Tomelty and the beginning of his relationship with Trudie Styler. Sting composed the song in 1982 while staying in Jamaica at Ian Fleming's Goldeneye estate, shortly after his separation from Tomelty. He has stated that he woke up in the middle of the night with the line "Every breath you take, I'll be watching you" in his head and wrote the song in about half an hour. Despite its seemingly romantic sound, Sting intended it to be about obsession and surveillance, reflecting the darker emotions he was experiencing at the time. He once said in an interview, "I think it's a nasty little song, really rather evil. It's about jealousy and surveillance and ownership." The recording sessions for "Synchronicity" were fraught with tension between the band members, particularly Sting and drummer Stewart Copeland. However, "Every Breath You Take" became the album's biggest hit and The Police's signature song. Guitarist Andy Summers contributed the distinctive arpeggiated guitar riff, which became a crucial element of the song's sound, though he reportedly had to be convinced by Sting to play it so sparsely. ðŸ“ˆ Chart Performance and Global Success #1 US & UK Hit Transatlantic Chart Topper Grammy Winner Song of the Year Biggest Hit of 1983 Billboard Year-End #1 Most Played Song Radio History (BMI) "Every Breath You Take" was an immense commercial success, becoming The Police's biggest hit and one of the most iconic songs of the 1980s. It topped the charts in both the United States and the United Kingdom. In the US, it spent eight weeks at #1 on the Billboard Hot 100 and was the best-selling single of 1983. In the UK, it spent four weeks at #1 on the UK Singles Chart . The song also reached #1 in Canada, Ireland, South Africa, and Israel, and was a Top 10 hit in numerous other countries worldwide. It won two Grammy Awards in 1984: Song of the Year (for Sting as songwriter) and Best Pop Performance by a Duo or Group with Vocal . It also won an Ivor Novello Award for Best Song Musically and Lyrically. "Every Breath You Take" has had remarkable longevity. In 2019, BMI recognized it as the most played song in radio history , surpassing "You've Lost That Lovin' Feelin'". Its enduring popularity, despite its often misunderstood lyrical content, solidifies its place as a classic of popular music. On the surface, they can be mistaken for a declaration of undying devotion, but a closer reading reveals a darker, more obsessive narrative. The song's structure is built on a series of parallelisms: "Every breath you take / Every move you make / Every bond you break / Every step you take / I'll be watching you." This relentless repetition of "every" action followed by "I'll be watching you" creates a sense of inescapable surveillance. Other lines like "Every single day / Every word you say / Every game you play / Every night you stay / I'll be watching you" further emphasize the narrator's constant, intrusive monitoring. The bridge reveals the narrator's pain and possessiveness: "Since you've gone I been lost without a trace / I dream at night I can only see your face / I look around but it's you I can't replace / I feel so cold and I long for your embrace / I keep crying baby, baby, please." This vulnerability, however, is framed by the overarching theme of surveillance, making the plea feel more like a demand rooted in obsession. The line "Oh, can't you see / You belong to me?" is the most explicit statement of the narrator's unhealthy possessiveness. The emotional resonance comes from this unsettling juxtaposition of a beautiful melody with lyrics that depict a controlling and potentially sinister mindset.
